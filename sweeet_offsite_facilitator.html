Your facilitator mindset for the day
  • Your job is to hold the container, not fill it. If you're talking more than Georgia, something is off.
  • Silence is productive. Count to ten after a hard question before rescuing. The answer is coming.
  • Name what you're noticing. "I notice we keep coming back to X" is a powerful move that doesn't require you to have the answer.
  • Protect the non-negotiables. When a decision conflicts with Georgia's list, point to it: "How does this land against what you wrote this morning?"
  • End on energy. Compress earlier sections before you cut the closing ritual. The December letter is non-negotiable.
  • Watch Georgia's energy specifically. She is coming out of cancer treatment. If she looks tired, check in: "Do you want a five-minute break?" That acknowledgment earns significant trust.
Know this before you walk in: your conflict of interest

You have a financial interest in the outcomes of this workshop. Georgia may not be fully aware of the extent of that. You need to be clean about this in how you facilitate.

  • Don't advocate for growth strategies that primarily create work for you. If a recommendation benefits you disproportionately, name it: "I should say this is work I could help with, so take my enthusiasm with that in mind."
  • Let the operational efficiency and retainer model conversations develop from the data and exercises, not from your direction.
  • Describe post-workshop deliverables as a natural extension of the work — not a pitch. "Here's what I'll build from today's outputs" is very different from "here's what I'd like to do next."

The cleanest version of the expanded engagement comes from Georgia feeling you served her interests completely today. Patience pays significantly better than impatience.

Welcome · 15 min
Opening and check-in
Opening script
Say this verbatim or close to it

"Today is not a presentation day. There are no slides to sit through and no performance reviews to survive. This is a working session — the value we produce depends entirely on how honest we're willing to be with each other."

"We're going to look at where the business actually is, where you actually want it to go, and build a plan that is realistic rather than aspirational. That means talking about some things that might feel uncomfortable. That's a feature, not a bug."

"Three ground rules: say what you actually think. Ask questions when something doesn't land. What's said in this room stays here until we decide together what to share."

Exercise — One word, one number (5 min)
Check-in5 minGo around the room

Each person gives one word describing how they feel about the business right now, and one number 1–10 for energy/optimism. Write everything on the whiteboard. Return to it at the close of day — the shift in words is usually worth noticing aloud.

Facilitator note

The numbers tell you a lot before Block 1 begins. A 4 from Georgia and an 8 from Mary signals misalignment that will surface throughout the day. Don't comment on it — just note it and let the day resolve it.

Block 1 · 45 min
Where we are
Honest reality check · No problem-solving yet
Block purpose

Ground everyone in shared, agreed-upon facts before any forward-looking conversation. Name what's true, validate it, and move on. This block does not solve problems — it names them clearly so everyone sees the same picture.

Step 1 — Financial chart walkthrough (10 min)
Data10 minThe margin story

Walk through the chart below. The headline is not the revenue dip — it's the margin compression. In 2018, 66 cents of every dollar came home. In 2025, 27 cents did. The business earned more gross revenue in 2025 than 2018 but Georgia took home significantly less.

Frame the chart this way

"This isn't a story about working less hard. It's a story about working differently. The business earned more gross revenue in 2025 than in 2018, but you took home significantly less. That gap is what we're here to close."

"2024 was a year that no business plan could have accounted for. We're not here to relitigate that. We're here to build forward from where we actually are."

Step 2 — Three structural findings (15 min)
Present, don't debate15 minThree findings to name
  • Finding 1: The referral engine has no steering wheel. All growth came through word of mouth. That's a strength — but it produced a client mix that's hard to deliver consistently and impossible to predict financially.
  • Finding 2: The highest-value work lives in one person's head. Strategic roadmap capability sits entirely with Georgia. That's a single point of failure and a delivery bottleneck.
  • Finding 3: The website service menu and the actual service menu don't fully match. The roadmap is marketed but not productized. This is an opportunity, not a criticism.
Watch for: Georgia defending current practices

Let her contextualize briefly, then redirect: "I hear you, and I want to capture that. Right now I just want us all to see the same picture before we talk about what to do about it."

Step 3 — Rose, Thorn, Bud (15 min)
Individual first, then share15 minRose, Thorn, Bud

Give each person 5 minutes to write their own answers privately on the worksheet (mirrored in their participant guide). Then go around and share, grouping common themes on the whiteboard. The Buds are the most valuable output — they often contain the seeds of the growth strategy.

Rose, Thorn, Bud — facilitator capture
What to do with the outputs

Write all Thorns on one whiteboard section and all Buds on another. Keep them visible all day. When Block 4 or 6 stalls, return to the Buds. The team often already knows the answer.

Block 1 must-have outputs
  • Shared agreement on the margin story — the gap between gross and net is the narrative
  • Three structural findings named and visible on the whiteboard
  • Rose/Thorn/Bud themes grouped and documented
Block 2 · 45 min
What Georgia wants
Life design before revenue targets · Most sensitive block of the day
How to open this block

"Before we talk about any numbers, I want to understand what you actually want your life to look like this year. The business should serve your life, not the other way around. Everything we build today starts here."

Step 1 — Ideal week (15 min)
Reflection15 minDesign the ideal work week

Walk Georgia through the prompts in her participant guide. Write the answers on the whiteboard. These become design constraints for the operating model, not aspirational fluff.

Step 2 — The real number (15 min)
Frame the income question exactly this way

"I'm not asking this to judge the number. I'm asking because the business needs to be designed around what you actually need, not a round number someone else would approve of. What do you need to take home personally in 2026 to feel genuinely well-compensated and financially secure?"

Math exercise10 minWork backwards from personal income

Once you have a personal income target, run the margin math on the whiteboard. Show three scenarios: 30% margin (current trajectory), 40% margin (recovery target), 45% margin (optimized). Let the gross revenue requirement emerge from the math. This usually produces a moment of clarity no arbitrary revenue target achieves.

Watch for: reluctance to name a number in front of the team

Georgia may be uncomfortable discussing personal income with Mary and Allison present. Offer an out: "We don't need to name a specific number in the room. Let's work with ranges, or you and I can do this math separately and bring just the gross revenue target back to the group."

Step 3 — Non-negotiables (10 min)
Context: "I just like my life"

Georgia already signaled she's not optimizing for maximum revenue. After a cancer diagnosis at 43, this is completely rational — not ambition avoidance. Treat it as the most honest design constraint in the room. The plan that honors that statement is the one she'll actually follow.

Block 2 must-have outputs
  • Ideal work week documented as a design constraint
  • Personal income target established (even as a range)
  • Gross revenue requirement derived mathematically at multiple margin scenarios
  • Non-negotiables list visible on the wall for the rest of the day

Step 2 — Pumpkin Plan (10 min)
Reference her own writing

Georgia has written about the Pumpkin Plan on the Marketing Sweeet blog. Say: "You already know this framework — you wrote about it. Let's use it as our lens here." That acknowledgment signals you've done your homework.

Step 3 — ICP build (20 min)
Group exercise20 minBuild the ideal client profile from the best clients

Use the high-value, high-joy quadrant as raw material. Work through the prompts in the participant guide. The output should be specific enough to write a prospecting list from. "Small businesses that need marketing help" is not an ICP. "Owner-led professional services firms, 10–50 employees, no internal marketing team, want to grow but don't know where to start" is.

Step 4 — Niche question (10 min)
Likely resistance: "I don't want to be boxed into a niche"
Reframe

"Choosing a focus isn't about turning people away. It's about knowing exactly who to say yes to first. You can still take the interesting client who doesn't fit. You just stop building your entire prospecting strategy around hoping one shows up."

Block 3 must-have outputs
  • Completed client matrix with quadrant placements — photograph the whiteboard
  • ICP with specific, usable parameters
  • Conscious decision on industry focus vs. staying horizontal
  • Bad-fit client criteria documented
  • Named clients to nurture, manage, and exit
☕ 10-minute break — non-negotiable. Get people standing. Check in with Georgia directly about energy before continuing.
Block 4 · 60 min
What you sell
Services · Pricing · Delivery reality · Revenue math
Step 1 — Service delivery audit (15 min)
Group exercise15 minWho can deliver what without Georgia?

Create a grid on the whiteboard: services across the top, team members down the side (Georgia, Mary, Allison, freelancers). For each service mark: delivers independently / delivers with oversight / cannot deliver without Georgia. When Georgia is the only checkmark in most columns, the group sees it together without anyone having to say it out loud.

Context note

Georgia does all paid social herself. Mary handles Google Ads only. Allison is not yet at a level to own strategy or client-facing delivery independently. This is the operational reality behind Finding 2.

Watch for: discomfort from Mary and Allison
Reframe for the team

"The goal isn't to replace what Georgia does. It's to build more capacity around her so the business can grow without burning anyone out. That's an opportunity for everyone in this room."

Step 2 — The roadmap gap (10 min)
Important context

The roadmap service is marketed on the website but does not exist as a productized offering. There is no template, no defined scope, no repeatable process, no price point. The Network Zen deck is the closest thing in the agency's files and it was built custom from scratch.

Frame it constructively

"The roadmap service is one of the most compelling things on the website — the perfect front door to a long-term engagement. But right now it exists as Georgia's expertise applied case-by-case. What would it take to give it a defined scope, deliverable, price, and process?"

Likely resistance: "I don't have time to build it"
Reframe

"You don't need to build the whole product before selling the first one. You just need to define scope, price, and deliverable. We can do that in a working session in a few hours. The first client you sell it to funds the time to refine it."

Don't try to build it today

The goal is agreement that productizing the roadmap is a 2026 priority, with a named owner and 60-day deadline. The actual build is a separate working session — a natural post-workshop engagement for you.

Step 3 — Retainer vs project math (15 min)
Math exercise15 minWhat does $200K actually require?

Scenario A (project-heavy): Avg project $5K. 40 projects/year = 3–4/month. High acquisition pressure, unpredictable revenue.

Scenario B (retainer-anchored): 8 clients at $1,500/month = $144K base. 4–5 projects fill the gap. Acquisition pressure drops, margin improves, revenue is predictable.

Ask: "Which feels more aligned with the ideal week we designed this morning?" Then: "Which current clients should be on retainer but aren't?"

Step 4 — Pricing audit (15 min)
Likely resistance: "Clients will leave if I raise prices"
Reframe

"The clients most likely to leave are the ones in the low-value quadrant of your client matrix. That's not a loss — that's a filter. Suggest a grandfathering approach: new clients go to the new rate, best existing clients get a 6-month grace period with advance notice."

Block 4 must-have outputs
  • Service delivery grid completed and photographed
  • Agreement to productize the roadmap — named owner, 60-day deadline
  • Target retainer client list and proposed structure
  • Revised pricing recommendations for 2026
  • Revenue model on whiteboard showing path to income goal

Step 3 — Content pillar audit (20 min)
Prospect lens audit20 minDoes the content answer what a prospect is actually asking?

Look at the current content mix through the eyes of a prospective client encountering the brand for the first time. One question guides everything: does this content mix answer the questions that prospect is actually asking? Then assign target percentages to each pillar for 2026.

Sensitive territory: the personal content conversation

The Sweeet Life content and personal posts may have felt like a lifeline during treatment. Don't critique specific posts. Handle it at the pillar level — if the framework agreed on doesn't include a bucket for personal relationship content, the guidance is implicit.

Reframe — the pivot that tends to land

"Georgia's cancer journey is actually extraordinary thought leadership material when it's framed as professional insight — what running a business through a health crisis taught you about resilience, delegation, and what actually matters. That story resonates with every small business owner who's faced a personal crisis while keeping clients happy. But it works as professional insight, not as personal processing."

How to close the percentage conversation

After the group fills in percentages, ask: "Where does the Sweeet Life content and personal update content fit in these four buckets?" Let the group answer. They'll usually land on the insight themselves without you having to deliver a verdict. If they don't, ask: "Which bucket does a post about a relationship breakup fall into?" The answer is obvious and lands without judgment.

Block 6 · 60 min
How you grow and close
Business development · Priorities · Commitments · Closing ritual
Step 1 — Intentional referral engine (15 min)
Group exercise15 minFour activations
  • Identify top referrers: Which clients have sent the most business? Assign a Q1 outreach action to each of the top five.
  • Make referring easy: Does a one-page ideal client description exist that a happy client could forward? If not, that's a 30-minute deliverable with significant ROI.
  • Ask explicitly: Script the ask: "We're looking to grow with one or two more clients this year. If you know anyone who'd benefit from what we do for you, we'd love an introduction."
  • Partner network: Map non-competing professionals serving the same client — accountants, coaches, web developers, PR firms.
Step 2 — Dream 10 (10 min)
Group exercise10 minName actual targets

"If you could add ten ideal clients in 2026, who would they be?" Name actual companies or specific types. For each: "Do we have any existing connection?" You'll find 3–4 are one or two introductions away. Those are the Q1 warm outreach targets.

Step 3 — Priorities synthesis (15 min)
How to run this

Refer back to the Decisions whiteboard. Name what the group agreed on through the day and consolidate into no more than five priorities. Push for specificity on Q1 actions — "do more business development" is not an action. "Georgia reaches out to three specific past clients about referrals in January" is.

Step 4 — The December letter (10 min)
Closing ritual10 minLook back from December 31

Give each person 5 minutes to write three sentences as if looking back from December 31, 2026. Ask 1–2 people to share. Close with the same one-word check-in from the morning. The shift in words from morning to end of day is usually worth noticing aloud.

After the day: your three deadlines
Within 48 hours: send Georgia a one-page summary of what was decided and committed to. Within 2 weeks: deliver the messaging map, content generator framework, and 2026 growth plan. Within 30 days: propose a working session to scope the roadmap product.
The workshop that gets documented becomes the plan. The one that doesn't becomes a memory.
