Today is not a presentation. There are no slides to sit through and no performance to give. This is a working session — everything we produce depends on how honest we're willing to be with each other.

We're going to look at where the business actually is, where you actually want it to go, and build a plan that is realistic rather than aspirational. That means talking about some things that might feel uncomfortable. That's a feature, not a bug.

Three ground rules: Say what you actually think. Ask questions when something doesn't land. What's said in this room stays here until we decide together what to share.

Where we are
Honest reality check · No problem-solving yet
The goal of this block is shared honesty about the starting point, not solutions. We're naming what's true so we can all see it clearly before we decide what to do about it.
The financial story: 2018–2025
DataThe margin story is the real headline

The chart below shows gross revenue (solid green) and net profit (dashed gold) from 2018 to 2025, with net margin percentage shown above each year. The headline isn't the revenue dip — it's the margin compression. In 2018, 66 cents of every dollar came home. In 2025, 27 cents did. The business is working harder and keeping less.

The three things we need to name
Structural findingsNot verdicts — observations
  • The referral engine has no steering wheel. All growth came through word of mouth. That's a strength — but it's produced a client mix that's hard to deliver consistently and impossible to predict financially.
  • The highest-value work lives in one person's head. Strategic roadmap capability currently sits entirely with Georgia. That's both a bottleneck and a ceiling on growth.
  • The website menu and the actual service menu don't fully match. Some services are marketed that haven't been fully productized yet. That's an opportunity, not a criticism.

Framework — The Pumpkin Plan lens
FrameworkPlant, weed, nurture

Using a framework you already know well — three filters for seeing the client roster clearly:

  • Plant the right seeds: What's the one thing Marketing Sweeet does better than anyone else? Focus everything there.
  • Weed out the losers: Which clients consume disproportionate time, energy, or emotional bandwidth?
  • Nurture the winners: Which clients, given more attention, could grow significantly or become your best referral sources?

Exercise — Revenue model: two scenarios
Math exerciseWhat does your number actually require?

We'll work through two scenarios on the whiteboard together. Which feels more aligned with your ideal week?

Scenario A — Project-heavy: Average project $5K. 40 projects/year to hit $200K = 3–4 per month. High acquisition pressure, unpredictable revenue.

Scenario B — Retainer-anchored: 8 clients at $1,500/month = $144K base. 4–5 projects fill the gap. Acquisition pressure drops. Margin improves. Revenue is predictable.

Our retainer opportunity
Exercise — Pricing audit
Group exerciseWhere are we leaving money on the table?

For each service: when did we last raise the price? Do clients ever push back, or do they accept without flinching? Does the price reflect the expertise required today — or what felt safe two or three years ago?

A consistent finding for agencies at this stage: pricing was set modestly during the credibility-building phase and never revisited. A 15–20% increase on retainer services often adds $20–30K annually without a single new client.

What we're building toward
  • Clear picture of what each team member can deliver independently
  • Agreement to productize the roadmap service (with owner and deadline)
  • Target retainer client list and proposed structure
  • Revised pricing recommendations for 2026
  • Revenue model showing the specific path to the income goal

Exercise — Making the referral engine intentional
Group exerciseFrom passive to active referrals
  • Identify your best referrers: Which current or past clients have sent the most business? When did you last actively thank them or check in?
  • Make it easy to refer: Does Marketing Sweeet have a simple one-page description of the ideal client that a happy client could forward to a colleague?
  • Ask explicitly: "We're looking to grow with one or two more clients this year. If you know anyone who'd benefit from what we do for you, we'd love an introduction."
  • Partner network: Who are non-competing professionals serving the same client? Accountants, business coaches, web developers, PR firms.
